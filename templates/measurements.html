Clinical Reference

Understanding
Your Measurements

A detailed guide to all 13 clinical features used by our model — what they measure, where they come from, and what their values mean.

⚠️ These values must come from a verified medical or clinical FNA biopsy report. Do not estimate or guess these numbers.

Where Do These Numbers Come From?

All 13 measurements originate from a procedure called a Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA) biopsy, followed by digital image analysis of the cell nuclei extracted.

You cannot measure these values at home. They are computed by pathology software from microscopic biopsy images and will appear in your clinical laboratory report.

Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA) is a minimally invasive biopsy procedure where a thin, hollow needle is inserted into a breast lump or suspicious tissue to extract a small sample of cells. The procedure takes only a few minutes and is usually done under local anaesthetic.

The extracted cells are placed on a glass slide and stained so that the nuclei (the control centres of the cells) become visible under a microscope. A pathologist then digitally images the sample at high magnification.

Specialised image analysis software — such as the Xcyt system used in the Wisconsin Breast Cancer Dataset that trained this model — then computes geometric and texture measurements of each cell nucleus. For each measurement, three values are recorded: the mean (average across all nuclei), the standard error (SE), and the worst (largest or most extreme value observed).

These computed values are what you enter into our screening tool.

The FNA Procedure — Step by Step

1

Preparation The area is cleaned and a local anaesthetic may be applied to minimise discomfort. The patient lies down or sits with the breast accessible.

2

Needle Insertion A thin needle (22–25 gauge) attached to a syringe is inserted into the lump. Ultrasound guidance is often used to ensure accuracy.

3

Cell Aspiration Gentle suction is applied as the needle is moved back and forth within the lump to collect a sample of cells and fluid.

4

Slide Preparation The aspirated material is spread on glass slides, fixed, and stained with special dyes to make cell nuclei visible under the microscope.

5

Digital Imaging A pathologist images the prepared slides at high magnification. The boundaries of each cell nucleus are traced digitally.

6

Feature Computation Software computes all 13 geometric and texture measurements from the traced nuclear boundaries and outputs them in the lab report.

Group 1 — Mean Measurements

The average value of each feature computed across all cell nuclei identified in the biopsy sample. These represent the typical characteristics of the nuclei.

1. Radius Mean
Average distance from nucleus centre to its boundary
radius_mean
Valid Range: 6.9 – 28.11 mm

The radius of a cell nucleus is computed as the mean distance from the centre of the nucleus to points on its perimeter. In practice, the pathology software traces the boundary of each nucleus and calculates the average of all radial distances from the centroid to the boundary points.

This is then averaged across all nuclei detected in the sample to produce radius_mean.

Unit
Micrometres (µm) — reported as a numeric value in your lab report
How to find in report
Listed as "Radius (mean)" or "Nuclear radius" in the FNA analysis section
What it tells the model: Malignant nuclei tend to be significantly larger than benign ones. A higher radius_mean is associated with more aggressive tumour behaviour. Values above ~17–18 are considered elevated.
2. Texture Mean
Standard deviation of grayscale pixel intensity values in the nucleus
texture_mean
Valid Range: 9.71 – 39.28

Texture describes how uniform or varied the internal appearance of a cell nucleus is in a grayscale microscopy image. It is computed as the standard deviation of the grayscale pixel intensity values within the nucleus boundary.

A low texture value means the nucleus appears uniform and smooth internally. A high texture value means the interior pixel intensities are highly variable — appearing mottled or irregular.

Unit
Grayscale standard deviation (unitless numeric)
How to find in report
Listed as "Texture (mean)" or "Nuclear texture" in FNA image analysis output
What it tells the model: Malignant nuclei often show more irregular internal texture due to abnormal chromatin distribution. Higher texture values suggest greater nuclear irregularity.
3. Perimeter Mean
Mean total boundary length of cell nuclei
perimeter_mean
Valid Range: 43.79 – 188.5

The perimeter is the total length of the boundary traced around each cell nucleus. It is computed by summing the distances between consecutive boundary points traced by the image analysis software around the nuclear edge.

The mean perimeter is the average of all nuclear perimeter lengths across the sample.

Unit
Micrometres (µm)
Relationship to radius
For a perfect circle: perimeter ≈ 2π × radius. Irregular shapes have larger perimeters relative to their radius.
What it tells the model: Larger and more irregular nuclei have higher perimeters. Malignant cells tend to have irregular, jagged boundaries which inflate the perimeter beyond what a smooth circular nucleus of the same size would have.
4. Area Mean
Mean cross-sectional area of cell nuclei
area_mean
Valid Range: 143.5 – 2501

The area is computed by counting the number of pixels enclosed within the traced nuclear boundary and converting to real-world units. It represents the cross-sectional area of the nucleus as seen in the 2D microscopy image.

This is one of the most directly interpretable features — it is essentially the size of the nucleus in the image plane.

Unit
Square micrometres (µm²)
Model importance
One of the most predictive single features — strongly correlated with malignancy
What it tells the model: Cancer cells frequently have enlarged nuclei — a hallmark of malignancy visible even in basic histopathology. Area is one of the strongest individual predictors in this dataset.
5. Compactness Mean
Ratio of perimeter² to area — measures shape regularity
compactness_mean
Valid Range: 0.019 – 0.34

Compactness is a shape metric computed as: (perimeter² / area) − 1.0. It measures how close a shape is to a perfect circle. A perfect circle has the minimum possible compactness value (0.0). As a shape becomes more irregular, elongated, or jagged, its compactness increases.

This feature is entirely derived from the perimeter and area — you will not see it directly in every report, but it is calculated automatically from those values.

Formula
(perimeter² ÷ area) − 1.0
Interpretation
0 = perfect circle. Higher values = more irregular shape
What it tells the model: Malignant nuclei tend to be less compact — more irregular and non-circular. High compactness values suggest the nucleus boundary is jagged or elongated rather than smooth.
6. Concavity Mean
Severity of concave (inward) portions of the nucleus boundary
concavity_mean
Valid Range: 0.0 – 0.42

Concavity measures the severity of indentations in the nucleus boundary — specifically, how deeply the boundary curves inward (concave portions). The software identifies sections of the boundary where the outline dips inward relative to a convex hull fitted around the nucleus.

A smooth, round nucleus has zero or near-zero concavity. An irregular nucleus with deep indentations has high concavity.

Computed from
The difference between the actual nuclear boundary and its convex hull (the tightest convex shape that encloses it)
Unit
Unitless ratio (0 = no concavities, higher = more indented)
What it tells the model: Malignant nuclei frequently show irregular, indented boundaries. High concavity is a strong marker of nuclear atypia — the abnormal shapes that pathologists look for in cancer diagnosis.
7. Concave Points Mean
Number of concave indentation points on nucleus boundary
concave points_mean
Valid Range: 0.0 – 0.2012

While concavity measures the depth/severity of indentations, concave points counts the number of concave portions on the nuclear boundary. Each distinct inward curve that dips below the convex hull is counted as one concave point.

This is then averaged across all nuclei in the sample to give the mean concave points value.

Difference from concavity
Concavity = how deep the indentations are. Concave points = how many distinct indentations exist.
Unit
Normalized count (number of concave segments ÷ total boundary segments)
What it tells the model: More concave points mean a more complex, lobulated nuclear boundary. This is one of the most diagnostically significant features — it directly reflects the irregular, multi-lobed shapes typical of malignant nuclei.

Group 2 — Worst Measurements

The largest or most extreme value observed across all cell nuclei in the sample. "Worst" does not mean worst outcome — it means the maximum recorded value for that feature.

8. Radius Worst
Largest nucleus radius observed in the sample
radius_worst
Valid Range: 7.93 – 36.04

Rather than the average, radius_worst captures the mean of the three largest radius values found in the sample. This highlights the most extreme nuclei — which pathologists often find diagnostically significant.

Computed as
Mean of the three largest nuclear radii in the sample
Always ≥
radius_mean — because it captures the upper extreme values
What it tells the model: Even if average nuclear size is moderate, very large outlier nuclei indicate more aggressive behaviour. The worst values often carry more predictive power than the means in differentiating benign from malignant tumours.
9. Texture Worst
Most irregular nuclear texture in the sample
texture_worst
Valid Range: 12.02 – 49.54

Texture_worst is the mean of the three highest texture (grayscale standard deviation) values found in the sample. It identifies the nuclei with the most variable, irregular internal appearance.

Computed as
Mean of the three highest nuclear texture values
Clinical relevance
Extremely irregular chromatin distribution is a hallmark of high-grade malignancy
What it tells the model: The most texturally irregular nuclei in a sample are often the most abnormal. High texture_worst values suggest the presence of highly atypical nuclei even if most nuclei appear relatively normal.
10. Perimeter Worst
Largest nuclear boundary length in the sample
perimeter_worst
Valid Range: 50.41 – 251.2

The mean of the three largest nuclear perimeter values in the sample. Large perimeters indicate either very large nuclei, or nuclei with highly irregular jagged boundaries — or both.

Computed as
Mean of the three largest nuclear perimeter values
Unit
Micrometres (µm)
What it tells the model: Large, jagged nuclear boundaries are characteristic of malignant cells. Perimeter_worst is particularly sensitive to the most extreme outlier nuclei in the sample.
11. Area Worst
Largest nuclear cross-sectional area in the sample
area_worst
Valid Range: 185.2 – 4254

The mean of the three largest nuclear area values in the sample. This is among the most powerful individual predictors of malignancy. Even a small number of very large nuclei in an otherwise normal-looking sample can be diagnostically significant.

Computed as
Mean of the three largest nuclear areas observed
Model importance
Consistently ranks as one of the top 3 most predictive features in this dataset
What it tells the model: The presence of even a few very large nuclei is a strong indicator of malignancy. Area_worst captures the most extreme nuclear enlargement in the entire sample.
12. Concavity Worst
Most severe boundary indentation in the sample
concavity_worst
Valid Range: 0.0 – 1.252

The mean of the three highest concavity values found in the sample — representing the nuclei with the deepest boundary indentations. Notice the range extends to 1.252, significantly higher than concavity_mean's maximum of 0.42, reflecting how extreme the most irregular nuclei can be.

Why range is wider
Worst values capture only the 3 most extreme nuclei, not the full average — so outliers pull the value much higher
Unit
Normalized ratio (higher = deeper boundary indentations)
What it tells the model: Very high concavity_worst values indicate the presence of nuclei with severely irregular, deeply indented boundaries — a key morphological marker of malignancy.
13. Concave Points Worst
Highest count of boundary indentation points in the sample
concave points_worst
Valid Range: 0.0 – 0.291

The mean of the three highest concave points values in the sample — representing the nuclei with the most distinct boundary indentations. This is consistently one of the most important features for distinguishing malignant from benign tumours.

Computed as
Mean of the three highest concave-points counts across all nuclei
Model importance
Frequently the single highest-importance feature in Random Forest analysis of this dataset
What it tells the model: Nuclei with many distinct concave points have extremely complex, multi-lobed shapes — a very strong indicator of malignancy. This feature often carries the highest predictive weight in the model.
