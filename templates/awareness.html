What Is Breast Cancer?

A clear, plain-language explanation of how breast cancer develops and why early awareness matters.

Breast cancer occurs when abnormal cells in the breast begin to grow and divide uncontrollably, forming a tumor. It most commonly develops in the cells lining the milk ducts (ductal carcinoma) or in the lobules that supply those ducts with milk (lobular carcinoma).

There are more than 18 known subtypes of breast cancer. Some — like ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) — are non-invasive and confined to the duct. Others are invasive and can spread to surrounding tissue and, if undetected, to distant organs through the bloodstream or lymphatic system.

About 5–10% of breast cancers are linked to inherited gene mutations such as BRCA1 and BRCA2. The remaining 90–95% are caused by a combination of lifestyle, hormonal, environmental, and age-related factors.

Why Awareness Matters

The difference between early and late detection can be life-changing. Awareness is what makes early detection possible.

Early Detection

Cancers found during screening are typically smaller and far less likely to have spread — making treatment simpler and more effective.

Silent Early Stages

Many early-stage breast cancers cause no pain or visible symptoms at all. Most tumors are discovered through routine screening, not symptoms.

Time Is Critical

The 5-year survival rate for localized (early-stage) breast cancer exceeds 99%. For cancer that has spread to distant organs, it drops significantly.

Reducing Fear

Awareness replaces fear with informed action. Understanding the signs and screening options helps people seek help sooner rather than waiting.

Impact of Early vs Late Detection

The stage at which breast cancer is detected has a profound effect on treatment complexity and quality of life.

Early Detection

Higher treatment success rate — often curable with surgery alone
Less aggressive treatment — may avoid chemotherapy entirely
Breast-conserving surgery (lumpectomy) is often possible
Better quality of life during and after treatment
Significantly lower emotional and financial burden

Late Detection

More complex, multi-stage treatment — often chemotherapy and radiation
Higher risk of cancer having spread to lymph nodes or distant organs
Mastectomy (full breast removal) more likely to be required
Lower survival rates and longer recovery periods
Significant emotional, physical, and financial stress

Screening & Early Detection Methods

Screening helps detect breast cancer before symptoms appear — when it is most treatable.

Self-Examination

Monthly self-checks help you learn what is normal for your body and notice any new changes promptly. Best done 7–10 days after your period.

Clinical Breast Exam

A trained healthcare professional examines the breasts for lumps or abnormalities. Recommended at least every 1–3 years for women in their 20s and 30s.

Mammography

Low-dose X-ray imaging of the breast — the most common and widely recommended screening test. Recommended every 1–2 years from age 40–50 onwards.

Ultrasound & MRI

Used in specific high-risk cases, or as a follow-up to a mammogram. MRI is especially useful for women with dense breast tissue or known BRCA mutations.

Prevention & Risk Reduction

While no method guarantees prevention, these lifestyle choices are associated with lower breast cancer risk.

Regular physical activity Maintain healthy weight Limit alcohol Avoid smoking Breastfeeding if possible Discuss HRT risks with doctor Regular screening from age 40 Genetic counselling if high-risk
