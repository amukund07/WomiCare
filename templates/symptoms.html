Awareness Guide

Recognizing
Warning Signs

Awareness of breast cancer symptoms can help you seek medical advice earlier. Most symptoms are not cancer — but any new change is worth checking.

Common Warning Signs

These are the most frequently reported symptoms. Most lumps and changes are benign — but any unusual change lasting more than a few weeks should be evaluated.

Lump in Breast or Underarm

A painless lump or thickening that feels different from surrounding tissue. Lumps that are hard, irregularly shaped, and painless are more likely to be concerning.

Change in Size or Shape

Noticeable swelling, shrinkage, or asymmetry in one breast that is not related to the menstrual cycle or recent weight change.

Skin Changes

Dimpling, redness, dryness, thickening, or skin resembling an orange peel (peau d'orange). Persistent redness covering the breast can indicate inflammatory breast cancer.

Nipple Changes

Inversion of the nipple, pain, unusual discharge (especially if bloody or clear without squeezing), or eczema-like scaly irritation around the areola.

Other Possible Symptoms

Less obvious signs that can still warrant attention from a healthcare professional.

  • Persistent breast pain unrelated to injury or menstrual cycle
  • Swelling in part of the breast, even without a distinct lump
  • Unusual warmth or heaviness in one breast
  • Swollen lymph nodes near the collarbone or underarm
  • Flattening or indentation of the breast surface
  • Veins on the breast becoming more visible
  • Unexplained discharge from the breast without squeezing

Important to Remember

Context matters. Most breast changes are not cancer.

  • Many changes are caused by non-cancerous conditions — infections, hormonal fluctuations, or benign lumps (fibroadenomas)
  • Most people with breast cancer have no symptoms at time of initial diagnosis — tumors are often found during routine screening
  • Breast cancer is more than 100 times more common in women than in men, but men can develop it too
  • Any new or persistent change lasting more than 2–3 weeks should be evaluated by a doctor regardless of pain level

Advanced Stage Symptoms

When breast cancer spreads beyond the breast (metastasis), it most commonly affects the bones, liver, lungs, and brain — each causing distinct symptoms.

Bone Metastasis

Progressive bone pain, swelling, and weakened bones that may lead to fractures. Often felt in the spine, hips, or chest.

Liver Metastasis

Abdominal pain or swelling, nausea, vomiting, and jaundice (yellowing of the skin or eyes).

Lung Metastasis

Persistent chest pain, shortness of breath, and a chronic cough that doesn't resolve with usual treatments.

Brain Metastasis

Persistent headaches, seizures, nausea, and disruptions to speech, vision, memory, or everyday behavior.

Known Risk Factors

Having one or more risk factors does not mean you will develop breast cancer — but awareness helps you have informed conversations with your doctor.

Older age Family history of breast cancer BRCA1 / BRCA2 gene mutations Alcohol consumption Obesity Lack of physical exercise Hormone replacement therapy Exposure to ionizing radiation Having children late or not at all Early onset of menstruation Prior history of breast cancer

About 5–10% of breast cancer cases result from an inherited genetic predisposition such as BRCA mutations.

When Should You
Consult a Doctor?

You don't need to wait for severe symptoms. If anything feels off, a conversation with your doctor is always the right move.

A lump or thickening that does not go away after 2–3 weeks, regardless of pain level

Any unexplained nipple discharge, especially if it is bloody or occurs without squeezing

Skin changes on the breast — redness, dimpling, or texture resembling orange peel — that persist

Symptoms that are worsening or spreading, even if they seem minor individually

You feel anxious or uncertain about any change — peace of mind from a professional is always worthwhile
